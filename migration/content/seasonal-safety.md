# Seasonal Safety

How to stay safe and stay informed through wildfire smoke, winter storms, and emergencies across Redmond, Terrebonne, and Crooked River Ranch.

## Sign up for Deschutes Alerts

This is the single most important thing you can do. Deschutes Alerts is the county's official emergency notification system. It covers Deschutes County and Crooked River Ranch, and sends evacuation notices, severe weather warnings, and other urgent alerts by call, text, and email for the locations you choose. Sign up or update your profile at deschutesalerts.org. Need help registering? Call the Sheriff's Office at 541-388-6501.

### Clean Air Shelters

During heavy smoke, find a cleaner-air space near you.

Search "Wildfire Related Clean Air Shelters."

### When smoke is heavy, protect yourself

- ▪
Stay inside if you can. Keep windows and doors closed.

- ▪
If it is too hot indoors, run air conditioning on recirculate, or move to a cooler, cleaner location.

- ▪
Use a HEPA air purifier if you have one.

- ▪
Cloth, dust, and surgical masks do not protect you from smoke. A NIOSH-approved N95 or P100 respirator can help adults, but does not come in children’s sizes.

- ▪
People most at risk: infants and young children, older adults, pregnant people, and anyone with heart or lung conditions.

### No monitor nearby? Use the 5-3-1 visibility check

Pick a landmark you know the distance to, and see how far you can clearly see.

## Through the cold months

### TripCheck Road Conditions

Real-time Oregon road conditions, closures, and cameras, including the Central Oregon passes.

### Warming Shelters

During cold weather, find the nearest warming or emergency shelter. The Homeless Leadership Coalition keeps the current list at cohomeless.org. The Shepherd’s House Redmond Center offers 24-hour shelter year round.

### Before winter driving

- ▪
Keep a cold-weather kit in your car: water, snacks, blankets, flashlight, phone charger, and basic first aid.

- ▪
Carry chains or traction tires, and know how to put them on.

- ▪
Check tripcheck.com before you leave, and tell someone your route and arrival time.

## A little prep goes a long way

### Build a 72-hour kit

Keep enough on hand for your household to manage for three days: water and food, medications, copies of important documents, phone chargers, cash, supplies for pets, and warm clothing. Store it somewhere easy to grab on the way out the door.

### Make a Plan

Agree on where your household will meet and how you will reach each other if you are apart. Plan for pets and for anyone who needs extra help. Practice it once so it feels familiar.

### Check on Neighbors

In any emergency, a quick knock on a neighbor’s door matters, especially for older adults and people who live alone. Trade phone numbers now, before you need them.

Wildfire-readiness tools, evacuation guidance, shelter information, and home-safety checklists from the Sheriff’s Office.
