03

Trust & reputation — no stars, by design

The deliberate anti-Yelp stance: residents still get honest signal, but a business can never be saddled with a permanent public score it can’t address. Reputation is built by showing up and being responsive — not by dodging a bad star.

Signal 1 · automatic

Presence & activity

Factual, earned badges: claimed & verified, posts weekly, follower count, member-since, “usually replies within a day.” No judgment — just whether the business shows up. Free-tier exposure that actually means something.

Signal 2 · positive-only

Recommendations

Residents recommend a place — a count that only goes up, so it can’t be review-bombed. Login required (no anonymous drive-bys); people who booked or earned loyalty are badged “verified customer” for weight. A new business reads “New to Compass,” never damned.

Signal 3 · private

Feedback & recovery

An unhappy customer is routed to “share feedback privately” with the owner — not “post a 1-star to the world.” The business gets to fix it; the resident feels heard. The managed version is the Inquiry Inbox (Pro) doing double duty — complaints become resolution.

No 1–5 average. Anywhere.

The star average is the element that gets weaponized, that’s lossy and unfair, and that rating-apps sell ads against. Removing it removes most of the trap in one move. This replaces the old “Reviews v2” slot on S5.

04

Numbers to set locally

Illustrative starting points to gut-check against the Redmond market — what local businesses will actually pay, and what these tools would otherwise cost standalone. Placeholders to validate, not pricing advice to follow blindly.

Member · profile + exposure
$15 / mo
range $12–19 · annual ≈ $150 (~2 mo free)

For the shop that just wants a complete, standout listing: full gallery, storytelling profile, unlimited/scheduled bulletins, full analytics + demand signals, follower tools, team access.

Pro · everything + tools
$29 / mo
range $25–35 · annual ≈ $290

Member + all operating tools: Bookings, Inquiry/Quote Inbox (with private-feedback recovery), Loyalty. A standalone booking tool alone often costs this much — so “all tools at $29” reads as the deal it is.

Free-tier limits
3 bulletins/mo · 5 photos
events unlimited · recommendations & presence signals included

Free stays generous enough to feel complete and earns real exposure — the reason every business lists, which is what keeps the directory whole.

Tier-count decision
2 paid tiers
alt: collapse to 1 (~$25, everything)

Two matches the “profile vs tools” split and gives a $15 on-ramp instead of forcing $29-or-nothing. Collapse to one only if the market finds two confusing. À la carte is gone — that’s the consolidation.

05

Build phasing — MVP first

Populate the directory and earn consumer usage before monetizing — you can’t sell exposure until residents are using the app. So v1 is the free directory + consumer app; the paid apparatus is a switch-on layer. ✓ marks screens already wireframed.

How to use this. This is the entitlement source of truth — when a screen gates a feature, it must match the matrix exactly. Build the data model so tier state (Free / Member / Pro) is read from one place (B6) and respected everywhere: caps in B1/B2, storytelling fields in B4, full analytics & demand signals in B5, the three tools (B7–B9) and their S5 surfaces.
